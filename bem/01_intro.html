Boundary Element Methods#

Boundary element methods reformulate boundary value problems as integral equations on the boundary. The unknown is a boundary density, and the solution in the domain is recovered from a layer potential.

Advantages and Challenges#

Advantages

Challenges

only the boundary is meshed

system matrices are dense

exterior domains need no artificial boundary

singular integrals need special quadrature

the solution can be evaluated throughout the domain

a fundamental solution must be known

From PDE to Boundary Integral Equation#

Let \(\Omega\) be the domain and \(\Gamma=\partial\Omega\) its boundary. Consider the Helmholtz problem

\[ \Delta u + \kappa^2 u = 0 \quad \text{in } \Omega, \qquad u = g \quad \text{on } \Gamma, \]

For a single layer representation, the solution is

\[ u(x) = (\widetilde V\rho)(x) = \int_\Gamma G_\kappa(x,y)\,\rho(y)\,d\sigma_y, \qquad x \in \Omega, \qquad G_\kappa(x,y) = \frac{e^{i\kappa |x-y|}}{4 \pi |x-y|}. \]

Here \(y\) is a source point on \(\Gamma\), \(x\) is the evaluation point, \(G_\kappa\) is the fundamental solution, and \(\rho\) is the unknown boundary density. The symbol \(\widetilde V\) denotes the potential in the domain.

Taking its trace on \(\Gamma\) gives the boundary operator \(V\) and the equation for the density:

\[ V\rho = g \quad \text{on } \Gamma. \]

Boundary Discretization#

Choose a finite dimensional boundary space and approximate the density by

\[ X_h = \operatorname{span}\{\varphi_1,\dots,\varphi_N\}, \qquad \rho_h = \sum_{j=1}^N c_j\varphi_j. \]

The Galerkin variational formulation is: find \(\rho_h \in X_h\) such that

\[ \langle V\rho_h, \eta_h \rangle_\Gamma = \langle g, \eta_h \rangle_\Gamma \qquad \forall\,\eta_h \in X_h, \]

Choosing \(\eta_h=\varphi_i\) gives the linear system

\[ \sum_{j=1}^N A_{ij}c_j=b_i, \qquad A\mathbf c=\mathbf b, \]

where

\[ A_{ij}=\int_\Gamma\int_\Gamma \varphi_i(x)G_\kappa(x,y)\varphi_j(y) \,d\sigma_y\,d\sigma_x, \qquad b_i=\int_\Gamma g(x)\varphi_i(x)\,d\sigma_x. \]

Why Fast Algorithms Are Needed#

Every boundary basis function interacts with every other one through \(G_\kappa(x,y)\). Therefore, \(A\) is dense:

  • storing the matrix costs \(O(N^2)\),

  • a direct matrix vector product costs \(O(N^2)\).

The fast multipole method keeps nearby interactions direct and groups distant interactions through expansions. This avoids forming the full dense matrix and accelerates its application.
